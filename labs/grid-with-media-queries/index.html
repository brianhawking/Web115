Can a number really be 'delicate'?

Prime numbers are pretty cool and usually a topic of discussion in your first pre-algebra class. They are numbers whose only factors are 1 and itself. Examples include 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, etc. After you do the homework and take the quiz, you probably never hear the term again. That isn’t true for certain mathematicians. In fact, some mathematicians devoted quite a bit of their research to studying prime numbers. One such number is the digitally delicate prime number. ...

A digitally delicate prime number is a prime number that has the following characteristic: if you change any of the digits in that prime number, it will no longer be prime. Take 17. If I change the 7 to a 9 I get 19 (which is still prime). So even though 17 is prime, it’s not digitally delicate. The smallest digitally delicate prime is 294,001. Change any of its digits to something else and you still won’t get a prime.

You might be thinking, “So? What’s the point?” Here’s the thing about some branches of mathematics. We like to study it. We come up with interesting questions just to see if we can answer them. There may not be an immediate use for this knowledge. It’s just a series of puzzles. In the 1970s, the mathematician Murray Klamkin was curious if such numbers existed. Why? Who knows. The question was stated and put out into the universe for someone to answer. Maybe sometime in the future these numbers will play a role in something incredible.

I'd like to order a 'Me'

Two parts iced tea one part lemonade, is what we today call an Arnold Palmer. It’s unlikely Arnold Palmer ‘invented’ the drink. I’m sure somewhere someone mixed the two much like how kids mix a variety of sodas. But without Arnold Palmer the drink would probably not be as popular as it is today ...

Arnold Palmer is quoted by the Washington post as saying “My wife made a lot of iced tea for lunch, and I said, ‘Hey, babe, I’ve got an idea. You make the iced tea and make a big pitcher, and we’ll just put a little lemonade in it and see how that works.’ We mixed it up, and I got the solution about where I wanted it and I put the lemonade in it. I had it for lunch after working on the golf course.”

Whenever he ordered the drink at the club he’d ask for iced tea with a third lemonade. Someone sitting nearby heard the order and asked for that “Arnold Palmer” drink. I find it so interesting that this small exchange gave birth to this drink.

Alan Turing

This story, sadly, is not funny nor entertaining. Nonetheless it’s one I’d like to share. Without the movie The Imitation Game most would never have known the name Alan Turing. I did a history project back in high school about how encryption and deception was used during WWII ...

Even my teacher knew very little of the history of the Enigma Machine and the man who’s credited for cracking it. Even less knew that Polish cryptologists broke the initial Enigma. Germany added additional levels of complexity that eventually led them to contact British Military Intelligence.

This isn’t about the success of Alan Turing. It’s about his tragic ending. In 1952 Alan Turing was arrested for homosexuality. It was illegal in Britain at the time. He was sentenced to 12 months of hormone therapy. A year after he completed his sentence, he was found dead from Cyanide poisoning. It was ruled a suicide. Some say it was due to his treatment because he was gay. Other’s suggest it was simply an accident. Nonetheless, due to his arrest in 1952 he was no longer able to work for Britain’s Government Communications Headquarters.

Even though he was able to successfully work outside the government I find it terribly tragic how his life ended. His work during WWII was classified. His contributions to ending the war were not known at this time. Not only did he study mathematics, cryptography, philosophy, but he also studied what we now call computer science and artificial intelligence. Where would we be in these areas today had Alan Turing be able to continue his work?

Think outside the box

During WWII planes would come back from missions all beat up. They asked a statistician to help figure out where to reinforce the planes so they'd lose less to gunfire. He pointed to a relatively bullethole free section and said 'reinforce here.'

"Why would we reinforce here? We barely see any of our planes coming back with damage here."

"Exactly," the statistician said. "The planes being damaged here are the ones not making it back."
