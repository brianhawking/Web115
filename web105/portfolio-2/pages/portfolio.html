Programmer - iOS

One of my biggest YouTube videos is on the Simplex Method (around 400,000 views). Since my students also struggle with this method, I decided to create two apps to help teach these techniques.

Simplex Method

This is called the Simplex Method, and it helps solve systems of linear inequalities (ie., linear programming). It's important to note that they DO NOT just solve the problems. I programmed it to let you practice the technique, make mistakes, and learn from them. I included a hint option to help when you get stuck. The first hint will be instructions on what you should do. The second and third hints will be more specific to your current situation.

Row Reduction

This one is called Row Reduction, and it helps students solve systems of equations. Normally you learn to solve a system of two equations / two variables using the substitution method or the addition method. Row reduction can solve these as well as much larger systems of equations. Like the Simplex Method app I programmed it to let you practice, make mistakes, and learn from them.

Programmer - JavaScript

This was one of the first games I made. When learning to program, I followed tutorials on how to make a simple tic tac toe game using JavaScript. Afterward, I wanted to make an Extreme Tic Tac Toe game. It involves two players trying to win the large Tic Tac Toe board by competing to win the smaller boards. The difficulty is when a player chooses a square in the small board the other player must move to the corresponding large square and play that board. You must try to work out a few moves ahead to try to force the second player to move you where you want.

I was trying to figure out some real-world applications to solve a system of equations. Though there are many, very few are fun. Lights out is a children's game where you click a circle to turn on or off that light. However, all adjacent lights will also turn on or off.

The feature that I share with my students is the solution option. It will indicate which lights must be pressed to successfully turn off all the lights. It involves solving a massive system of equations. For example, a 3x3 grid requires us to solve a system with 9 equations and 9 variables. An 11x11 grid requires us to solve a system of equations with 121 equations and 121 variables. The hardest system we teach in any algebra class is 3 equations and 3 variables. So imagine how hard 121 equations/variables would be.
